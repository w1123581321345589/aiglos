Three things that were broken before and are now fixed at the architectural level.

---

**1. The false positive death spiral is broken.**

Before Tracks A-C, every `~/.aws/credentials` read fired T19. Every time. For every agent. A DevOps agent that runs 40 deployments a day and reads credentials on each one got 40 T19 blocks. The team either approved all 40 individually (fatigue), wrote a permanent allow-list (security hole), or turned off Tier 3 (the outcome that actually happened at CrowdStrike's early competitors).

After Track A, the behavioral baseline sees that this DevOps agent reads credentials in 100% of its sessions. It's in the fingerprint. When session 101 fires T19, the baseline contribution to the composite score is near zero — this is normal behavior for this agent. The rule still fires at the call level. The campaign analyzer still looks for the sequence. But the session-level anomaly score reflects reality: this is a clean session.

After Track C, even if the rule keeps blocking, the fifth block surfaces one policy proposal. One human decision. The DevOps agent never gets blocked again. The signal is a policy change, not an alarm.

The product went from "security tool your team disabled after two weeks" to "security tool that learns your deployment."

---

**2. New deployments are no longer flying blind.**

Before Track B, a new deployment had zero data. The intent predictor needed 5 sessions before it made any predictions. The behavioral baseline needed 20. For the first weeks of a new deployment, Aiglos was essentially a static rule engine — the same as anything else on the market.

After Track B, a new deployment warm-starts from a global prior representing what has been observed across every contributing deployment. Day one, session one — the predictor already knows that T19 sequences frequently precede T37 in the wild, that RECON_SWEEP patterns have a 73% correlation with subsequent FIN_EXEC attempts, that certain injection sequences spanning three tool calls are being seen at three other companies right now.

The cold-start problem is not reduced. It is eliminated. The product that ships to customer two is fundamentally better than the product that shipped to customer one, without customer two doing anything differently.

---

**3. The product stops fighting its users and starts working with them.**

Before, the security UX had two modes: block everything (alert fatigue, tool disabled) or allow everything (security theater). The webhook approval pattern that shipped was technically correct and operationally useless — engineers approved everything to unblock their work, which is the only rational response to per-action interruption.

After Track C, the UX is: the system watches, accumulates evidence, and when it has enough — five blocks, consistent pattern, no incidents, baseline confirmation — it surfaces one decision. "This agent has been blocked doing this thing seven times. Should we permanently lower the tier?" That is a different kind of question. It has a real answer. It takes ten seconds instead of firing a webhook at 2am. The decision persists. The engineer engages with security as a policy function rather than an interruption function.

The deeper change: the product now distinguishes between "this looks like an attack" and "this agent always does this." Those were identical signals before. They are now two different things with different responses.

---

**What this means for the pitch.**

The product before Tracks A-C was a detection engine. The same category as Sysdig, Falco, Datadog Security. Rule-based, session-scoped, agent-agnostic. Better rules than most, but the same fundamental product.

The product after is a security intelligence platform that improves with scale, learns each deployment's behavior, and makes policy decisions rather than firing alarms. That is a different category. CrowdStrike didn't win because it had better signatures. It won because every endpoint made every other endpoint smarter, and the product got harder to compete with every day it was deployed.

Aiglos now has that architecture. The network effect exists. The behavioral fingerprint is a local moat — nobody can replicate what the baseline knows about your specific deployment. The federation layer is a global moat — the longer Aiglos is deployed across the network, the better the global prior gets, and the worse any point-in-time competitor looks.

The question for the investor conversation is no longer "does this detect threats." It is "does this get permanently better the more it's deployed." The answer is now yes, demonstrably, with working code.
